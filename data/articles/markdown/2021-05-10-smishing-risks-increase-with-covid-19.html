Those annoying text messages are popping up on smartphones at an alarming rate and with increasingly deceptive messages that prey on our COVID-19 anxieties and new online habits, however, they are more than an annoyance, these text scams are a real threat to your data, your device, and potentially your organization’s network.

Smishing, which combines SMS and phishing, is not new; however, as we provide our phone number to communicate with more and more legitimate online companies, access to our cell phones becomes easier to access for those looking to access or sell your data or just wreak havoc on your devices.

According to the Federal Communications Commission (FCC), scammers use links in text messages to install malware on your phone or send you to a webpage to trick you into providing personal information, including social security numbers or banking information. Because scammers are clever and count on emotion, panic and fear to get you to click before thinking, fake COVID-19 text messages are especially prevalent right now. The FCC warns, “Do not click on links in texts related to the virus, and check cdc.gov/coronavirus for the most current information.”

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has also posted an alert about a COVID-19 vaccines scam survey, which explains how people have received emails and text messages asking them to complete a limited-time survey about the Pfizer, Moderna or AstraZeneca vaccine in exchange for a “free reward,” for which they’re asked to pay shipping fees.

Other text scams are impersonating government agencies. The FCC reports that a text scam is circulating that claims to be from the “FCC Financial Care Center” offering $30,000 in COVID-19 relief. And with so much online ordering and packages being delivered, an especially sneaky text came into my phone saying it was from the United States Postal Service (USPS) and they had a package and that I needed to click the link to claim the package. If I hadn’t had my morning coffee, I might have clicked it!

The list goes on and on (and these are just the ones I personally received over the past week) – a free one-year subscription to Hulu because of the pandemic, lose those COVID pounds, receive an Amazon reward, your [insert whatever company you subscribe to that seems legit) account was locked because payment was declined. Someone even tried to recruit me to join the Army (although, I think that one was legit, and I wanted to tell them there is no way I would ever get through boot camp due to those extra pandemic pounds). Netflix has posted an entire webpage to address text scams.

While most of us are aware of the signs of more traditional email phishing attempts – which are often caught by our spam filters along with some messages we might actually want to receive – smishing seems so much more invasive and personal. It is how we communicate with friends, family, and colleagues because it cuts through the email clutter and lands right on someone’s device they have on them.

So even if you are pretty savvy about not providing personal information with a phishing attempt, here is why it is so important to avoid clicking on the links:

You can inadvertently download malicious software that could infect your smartphone.

If you connect your phone to your computer (to transfer files, etc.), it could infect that computer.

If your infected computer is connected to your organization’s network, the malware could then be spread depending on your organization’s malware and virus protection.

The FCC offers the following tips to help prevent being smished:

Do not respond to calls or texts from unknown numbers, or any others that appear suspicious.

Never share your personal or financial information via email, text messages, or over the phone.

Be cautious if you’re being pressured to share any information or make a payment immediately.

Scammers often spoof phone numbers to trick you into answering or responding. Remember that government agencies will never call you to ask for personal information or money.

Do not click any links in a text message. If a friend sends you a text with a suspicious link that seems out of character, call them to make sure they weren’t hacked.

We listed a few ways below to help filter unwanted text messages.

On your phone

Your phone may have an option to filter and block messages from unknown senders or spam. Here’s how to filter and block messages on an iPhone and how to block a phone number on an Android phone.

Through your wireless provider

Your wireless provider may have a tool or service that lets you block calls and text messages. Check ctia.org, a website for the wireless industry, to learn about the options from different providers.

With a call-blocking app

Some call-blocking apps also let you block unwanted text messages. Go to ctia.org for a list of call-blocking apps for Android, BlackBerry, Apple, and Windows phones. You can also search for apps online. Check out the features, user ratings, and expert reviews.

If you get an unwanted text message, the FTC lists three ways to report it:-

Report it on the messaging app you use. Look for the option to report junk or spam. How to report spam or junk in the Messages app How to report spam on an Android phone

Copy the message and forward it to 7726 (SPAM), which is available no matter what phone you use.

Report it to the Federal Trade Commission at ftc.gov/complaint.

According to the New York Times, reporting spam texts could help by deterring some scammers since “most carriers participate in a service that will use this information to try and block future spammers.” The report also notes that when Android users report text messages as spam, “this sends the message to Google as well as your carrier, so Google can improve their spam blocking filters.”
